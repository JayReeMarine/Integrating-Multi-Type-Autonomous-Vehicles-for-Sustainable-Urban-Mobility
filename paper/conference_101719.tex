\begin{abstract}
This paper addresses the energy inefficiency of single-occupancy vehicles by introducing a novel cooperative autonomous vehicle system where smaller Passive Vehicles (PVs) can be physically towed by larger Active Vehicles (AVs) during shared highway segments. Unlike traditional platooning that relies solely on aerodynamic drafting, our approach enables complete PV propulsion energy elimination during attached segments. We formulate the platoon formation problem as a constrained optimization problem on a one-dimensional highway model and propose two algorithms: a Greedy Maximum-Weight Matching algorithm and an Iterative Linear Assignment (ILA) algorithm using the Jonker--Volgenant method. A key innovation is the multi-segment matching capability, allowing a single PV to be towed by multiple AVs across different route segments. Experimental evaluation on synthetic scenarios with up to 400 PVs and 80 AVs demonstrates that both algorithms achieve 25--52\% distance coverage (proportional to energy savings under constant per-distance propulsion) depending on capacity, with ILA providing per-iteration optimality---i.e., an optimal solution to the bipartite subproblem constructed at each iteration---while preserving capacity and temporal feasibility across multi-segment assignments. While global optimality for the full multi-segment problem remains an open question, ILA consistently matches or exceeds greedy performance across all tested scenarios, and runtime differences are reported as empirical observations.
\end{abstract}

Single-occupancy travel remains a major barrier to sustainable urban mobility. U.S. National Household Travel Survey data show that drive-alone commuting dominates trip demand~\cite{usdot2024}, while the transportation sector contributes about 29\% of U.S. greenhouse gas emissions~\cite{epa2023}. This inefficiency is pronounced when many vehicles traverse overlapping routes but still consume propulsion energy independently.

To address this, we introduce a new problem that we call \textit{dynamic platoon formation}. We consider two classes of autonomous vehicles: larger \textit{Active Vehicles (AVs)} capable of physically towing smaller vehicles, and smaller \textit{Passive Vehicles (PVs)} that can be attached to AVs during shared highway segments. Unlike drafting-based platooning, which yields modest aerodynamic savings while vehicles remain physically separated, our model assumes mechanical coupling: when a PV is attached to an AV, the PV's own propulsion on that segment is \emph{completely eliminated}. Given a set of AVs and PVs with known routes, entry times, and speeds, the goal is to \textbf{maximize the total PV distance covered by towing} subject to point-wise AV capacity constraints and temporal synchronization requirements. We use covered distance as a proxy for PV-side propulsion-energy reduction, and reserve physics-level load modeling for future work.

Figure~\ref{fig:intro_concept} illustrates a concrete example. Three PVs ($T1$, $T2$, $T3$) travel along a shared highway, and two AVs ($A_1$, $A_2$) each have towing capacity~2. In the depicted solution, $T1$ is towed by $A_1$ over $[15,60]$; $T2$ is first towed by $A_1$ and then handed off to $A_2$ at $x\!=\!70$, demonstrating \textit{multi-segment matching}---a single PV served by multiple AVs across different route segments; and $T3$ is towed by $A_2$ over $[60,92]$. Between $x\!=\!20$ and $x\!=\!60$, $A_1$ simultaneously tows $T1$ and $T2$, reaching its capacity of~2. Solid segments denote towed (propulsion-free) travel; dashed segments denote self-driving.

Prior work spans platooning~\cite{tsugawa2016review,bergenhem2012overview}, ridesharing and routing~\cite{furuhata2013ridesharing,agatz2012optimization,cordeau2007dial}, and assignment optimization~\cite{kuhn1955hungarian,munkres1957algorithms}. However, none of these lines of work models physical towing that combines multi-segment PV coverage with point-wise AV capacity constraints in a single framework. Our formulation targets that gap.

The resulting optimization problem---maximizing total covered PV distance under overlap, capacity, and temporal synchronization constraints---is a multi-segment matching problem that generalizes the generalized assignment problem, which is known to be NP-hard~\cite{cattrysse1992survey}. Exact solutions are therefore intractable for realistic fleet sizes. We propose two approximate solution strategies. First, a \textit{Greedy Maximum-Weight Matching} algorithm iteratively selects the highest-benefit feasible AV--PV towing pair and commits to it, repeating until no feasible pairs remain. This approach is fast but myopic. Second, an \textit{Iterative Linear Assignment (ILA)} algorithm decomposes the multi-segment problem into a sequence of bipartite matching subproblems: at each iteration, it constructs a cost matrix from current PV states, expands each AV into virtual slots to encode capacity, solves the resulting Linear Sum Assignment Problem (LSAP) optimally via the Jonker--Volgenant method~\cite{scipy2020}, updates PV states to reflect newly covered segments, and repeats until no further feasible assignments exist. For tractability, we evaluate both algorithms under a one-dimensional highway abstraction.

This paper makes three main contributions. First, we formalize the dynamic platoon formation problem with multi-segment matching and point-wise AV capacity constraints. For tractability, we instantiate and evaluate this formulation on a one-dimensional highway abstraction. Second, we develop and compare two approximate solution strategies: a greedy matching algorithm and an Iterative Linear Assignment (ILA) method with virtual-slot capacity encoding. Third, we provide a controlled experimental study (50--80 AVs, 200--400 PVs) showing substantial coverage savings, with ILA consistently matching or exceeding greedy performance across all tested scenarios.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Platooning and Drafting}

Platooning studies report 10--20\% fuel savings primarily through aerodynamic drafting~\cite{tsugawa2016review,bergenhem2012overview,shladover2009cooperative}. Vehicles remain physically separated, coordinating spacing and speed via cooperative adaptive cruise control~\cite{ploeg2011design}. More recent work has examined platoon coordination on highway networks, including scheduling when and where vehicles should form platoons to minimize fuel consumption~\cite{drexl2012synchronization}. Our setting differs fundamentally: we model physical towing rather than aerodynamic drafting. When a PV is attached, its propulsion is fully eliminated, and the matching decisions involve dynamic attach/detach operations under point-wise capacity constraints---a problem structure absent from standard drafting formulations.

\subsection{Ridesharing and Vehicle Coordination}

Ridesharing and dial-a-ride methods optimize passenger-to-vehicle assignment with timing and capacity constraints~\cite{furuhata2013ridesharing,agatz2012optimization,alonso2017demand,cordeau2007dial}. Dynamic trip-vehicle assignment approaches~\cite{alonso2017demand} and multi-agent intersection coordination~\cite{dresner2008multiagent} address related resource-allocation problems in transportation. However, in our problem the decision variable is vehicle-to-vehicle coupling over road segments, not passenger placement. Capacity is enforced point-wise along each AV's path (the set of attached PVs changes at every coupling/decoupling point), which differs from conventional seat-capacity or intersection-slot constraints.

\section{Problem Formulation}

We formalize the platoon formation problem on a simplified one-dimensional highway model. This abstraction captures the essential optimization trade-offs while enabling tractable analysis. Extension to general road networks is discussed in Section~\ref{sec:future}.

\subsection{System Model}

We consider a coordinated platoon formation system operating on a unidirectional highway segment of length $L$. The system comprises two distinct vehicle classes.

\begin{definition}[Active Vehicle]
An Active Vehicle (AV) $a_i \in \mathcal{A}$ is a larger autonomous vehicle capable of towing multiple smaller vehicles. Each AV is characterized by the tuple $(e_i^a, x_i^a, C_i, t_i^a, v_i^a)$ where $e_i^a, x_i^a \in [0, L]$ are entry and exit points, $C_i \in \mathbb{Z}^+$ is the towing capacity, and $t_i^a, v_i^a$ are the entry time and constant speed.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Passive Vehicle]
A Passive Vehicle (PV) $p_j \in \mathcal{P}$ is a smaller autonomous vehicle that can be towed by AVs. Each PV is characterized by the tuple $(e_j^p, x_j^p, t_j^p, v_j^p)$ where $e_j^p, x_j^p \in [0, L]$ are entry and exit points, and $t_j^p, v_j^p$ are entry time and self-driving speed.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Shared Path and Energy Model}

For an AV $a_i$ and PV $p_j$, the \textit{shared path} is the overlap of their routes:
\begin{equation}
    d_{ij} = \max(0, \min(x_i^a, x_j^p) - \max(e_i^a, e_j^p))
    \label{eq:shared}
\end{equation}
This quantity is the physically towable overlap between the two routes. It is needed to define both feasibility and the towing benefit of a candidate segment.

The coupling point $cp_{ij}$ (where the PV attaches) and decoupling point $dp_{ij}$ (where the PV detaches) are defined as $cp_{ij} = \max(e_i^a, e_j^p)$ and $dp_{ij} = \min(x_i^a, x_j^p)$, respectively.

The energy saving when PV $p_j$ is towed by AV $a_i$ is proportional to shared distance, i.e., $S_{ij} = \alpha d_{ij}$, where $\alpha$ is an energy coefficient. We set $\alpha = 1$, so saving equals covered distance.

\subsection{Multi-Segment Matching}

A key innovation of our formulation is \textit{multi-segment matching}: a single PV can be matched to multiple AVs across different segments of its route.

\begin{definition}[Segment Assignment]
A segment assignment for PV $p_j$ to AV $a_i$ is a tuple $(p_j, a_i, s, e)$ where $[s, e] \subseteq [e_j^p, x_j^p]$ is the segment during which $p_j$ is towed by $a_i$.
\end{definition}

For PV $p_j$, its route from $e_j^p$ to $x_j^p$ can be partitioned into covered segments (towed by AVs, thus saving propulsion energy) and uncovered segments (self-driven, with no towing-based saving).
This is necessary because one AV often covers only part of a PV route, so multiple feasible handoffs may be required to increase total coverage.

\subsection{Optimization Problem}

Let $\mathcal{X}$ denote the set of all segment assignments $(p_j, a_i, s, e)$ where PV $p_j$ is towed by AV $a_i$ over segment $[s, e]$. The decision variables are the selected segment assignments in $\mathcal{X}$. The optimization problem is:

\begin{align}
    \max \quad & \sum_{(p_j, a_i, s, e) \in \mathcal{X}} (e - s) \label{eq:obj}\\
    \text{s.t.} \quad & \left|\{(p_j, a_i, s, e) \in \mathcal{X} : x \in [s, e]\}\right| \leq C_i \notag\\
    & \qquad \forall i \in [N], \forall x \in [e_i^a, x_i^a] \label{eq:capacity}\\
    & \text{Segments for each } p_j \text{ are pairwise disjoint} \label{eq:nonoverlap}\\
    & (e - s) \geq L_{min} \quad \forall (p_j, a_i, s, e) \in \mathcal{X} \label{eq:minlen}
\end{align}
The objective \eqref{eq:obj} maximizes total covered PV distance, which is our proxy for PV propulsion-energy reduction.

\textbf{Constraint Interpretation:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Point-wise Capacity} \eqref{eq:capacity}: At any position $x$ along AV $a_i$'s route, the number of simultaneously towed PVs cannot exceed $C_i$. This is enforced point-wise (not only per AV globally) because attachments change along the route as PVs couple and decouple.
    \item \textbf{Non-overlap} \eqref{eq:nonoverlap}: A PV cannot be towed by two AVs simultaneously at any position.
    \item \textbf{Minimum length} \eqref{eq:minlen}: Each towing segment must exceed $L_{min}$ to justify coupling overhead.
\end{itemize}

Constraint \eqref{eq:capacity} applies at every position $x$, but the simultaneous tow count is a step function that changes only at coupling/decoupling endpoints. It therefore suffices to check capacity only at these \textit{critical points}; the following lemma formalizes this.

\begin{lemma}[Critical Points Sufficiency]
\label{lem:critical}

Fix an AV $a_i$ and let $\mathcal{X}_i = \{(p_j, a_i, s, e) \in \mathcal{X}\}$ be the assignments to $a_i$. Define the critical set $\mathcal{C}_i = \{e_i^a, x_i^a\} \cup \{s, e : (p_j, a_i, s, e) \in \mathcal{X}_i\}$. Then the point-wise capacity constraint \eqref{eq:capacity} holds for all $x \in [e_i^a, x_i^a]$ if and only if it holds for all $x \in \mathcal{C}_i$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The number of PVs towed by AV $a_i$ is a step function over $x \in [e_i^a, x_i^a]$, incrementing at segment start points $s$ and decrementing at segment end points $e$. Between consecutive critical points, the count is constant. Thus, capacity satisfaction at all critical points implies satisfaction everywhere.
\end{proof}

This lemma is applied in both of our algorithms (Algorithms~\ref{alg:greedy} and~\ref{alg:hungarian}): instead of verifying the capacity constraint at every continuous point along an AV's route, each algorithm checks capacity only at the finite set of coupling and decoupling endpoints, reducing the feasibility check from a continuous verification to a discrete one.

\subsection{Assumptions and Temporal Constraints}

When temporal synchronization is required at a coupling point $cp$:
\begin{equation}
    |t_i^a(cp) - t_j^p(cp)| \leq \tau
    \label{eq:time}
\end{equation}
where $t_i^a(cp) = t_i^a + (cp - e_i^a)/v_i^a$ is when AV $a_i$ reaches the coupling point, and $t_j^p(cp)$ is when PV $p_j$ reaches the same point under its current planned trajectory. In our experiments, we use $\tau = 5$ time units with vehicle speeds varying $\pm 20\%$ around a baseline.
Constraint \eqref{eq:time} captures temporal feasibility: even with spatial overlap, coupling is invalid if the two vehicles cannot meet within tolerance.

\textbf{Assumption (Coupled Speed):} During an attached segment, the PV follows the AV speed. Decoupling occurs when the shared segment ends or when either vehicle exits its route.

Overall, the formulation is defined by: decision variables (selected segment assignments), objective \eqref{eq:obj}, and feasibility constraints including overlap, point-wise capacity, non-overlap, minimum segment length, and temporal synchronization.

\section{Proposed Algorithms}
\label{sec:algorithms}

The dynamic platoon formation problem~\eqref{eq:obj}--\eqref{eq:minlen} generalizes the generalized assignment problem (GAP), which is NP-hard~\cite{cattrysse1992survey}: each PV can be viewed as a job, each AV position interval as a resource bin, and the point-wise capacity constraint~\eqref{eq:capacity} as a bin-capacity limit. Because our formulation additionally involves multi-segment assignments, temporal synchronization, and position-dependent capacity, the problem is at least as hard as GAP. Exact solutions are therefore intractable for realistic fleet sizes. We propose two approximate algorithms: a greedy matching algorithm (Section~\ref{sec:greedy}) and an efficient Iterative Linear Assignment algorithm (Section~\ref{sec:ila}).

\subsection{Greedy Maximum-Weight Matching}
\label{sec:greedy}

The greedy algorithm iteratively selects the single feasible AV--PV assignment with the highest towing distance, commits to it, updates vehicle states, and repeats. While intuitive and straightforward to implement, this approach is myopic: each commitment is irrevocable and may preclude globally superior combinations. Algorithm~\ref{alg:greedy} operates in three phases:

\textbf{Phase 1 (Initialization):} For each PV $p_j$, initialize state $(\ell_j, \hat{t}_j) \leftarrow (e_j^p, t_j^p)$. For each AV, track current assignments for capacity checking.

\textbf{Phase 2 (Candidate Generation):} Enumerate all feasible (AV, PV, segment) tuples where: (1) overlap with uncovered interval $[\ell_j, x_j^p] \geq L_{min}$, (2) AV has remaining capacity at all critical points per Lemma~\ref{lem:critical}, (3) time constraint~\eqref{eq:time} is satisfied.

\textbf{Phase 3 (Greedy Selection):} Select maximum-saving candidate, update PV and AV states, repeat until no feasible candidates remain.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Greedy Multi-AV Platoon Matching}
\label{alg:greedy}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE AVs $\mathcal{A}$, PVs $\mathcal{P}$, minimum length $L_{min}$
\ENSURE Assignments $\mathcal{X}$, total saving
\STATE Initialize $(\ell_j, \hat{t}_j) \leftarrow (e_j^p, t_j^p)$ $\forall j$
\STATE Initialize $assignments[i] \leftarrow \emptyset$ $\forall i$
\STATE $\mathcal{X} \leftarrow \emptyset$, $total \leftarrow 0$
\REPEAT
    \STATE $candidates \leftarrow$ all feasible (AV, PV, segment) tuples
    \IF{$candidates = \emptyset$}
        \STATE \textbf{break}
    \ENDIF
    \STATE Sort $candidates$ by saving (descending)
    \STATE $(saving, a_i, p_j, cp, dp) \leftarrow candidates[0]$
    \STATE $\mathcal{X} \leftarrow \mathcal{X} \cup \{(p_j, a_i, cp, dp)\}$
    \STATE Update $(\ell_j, \hat{t}_j)$ via~\eqref{eq:state_pos}--\eqref{eq:state_time}
    \STATE Update $assignments[i]$
    \STATE $total \leftarrow total + saving$
\UNTIL{no candidates}
\RETURN $\mathcal{X}$, $total$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\textbf{Complexity.} Each iteration produces $O(NM)$ candidates and sorts them in $O(NM \log(NM))$. Since greedy commits one assignment per iteration and the number of accepted segments is at most $O(NM)$, the total worst-case complexity is $O((NM)^2 \log(NM))$.

\subsection{Iterative Linear Assignment (ILA) Algorithm}
\label{sec:ila}

A single assignment solve is insufficient for multi-segment matching: accepting a towing segment changes PV residual intervals and arrival times, altering future pairing benefits. ILA addresses this by solving a sequence of bipartite matching subproblems. Each iteration constructs a weighted bipartite graph from current PV states, solves the resulting Linear Sum Assignment Problem (LSAP) optimally via the Jonker--Volgenant method~\cite{kuhn1955hungarian,munkres1957algorithms,burkard2012assignment}, updates PV states to reflect newly covered segments, and repeats until no further feasible assignments exist. AV capacity is encoded through \textit{virtual slot expansion}: each AV $a_i$ with capacity $C_i$ is replicated into $C_i$ identical slots in the bipartite graph.

\subsubsection{Virtual Slot Expansion and Bipartite Graph}

To encode point-wise capacity, each AV $a_i$ is expanded into $C_i$ virtual slots, producing $K = \sum_{i=1}^{N} C_i$ left-side nodes. The $M$ PVs (with their current uncovered intervals) form the right-side nodes. Figure~\ref{fig:bipartite} illustrates this construction using the toy example from Section~\ref{sec:toyexample}.

\subsubsection{Cost Matrix Construction}

The cost matrix $\mathbf{W} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times M}$ encodes the bipartite graph. For each virtual slot $k$ belonging to AV $a_i$ and each PV $p_j$:
\begin{equation}
W_{kj} = \begin{cases}
-d_{ij}^{curr} & \text{if feasible (overlap } \geq L_{min}\text{, time ok)} \\
+\infty & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $d_{ij}^{curr} = \max(0, \min(x_i^a, x_j^p) - \max(e_i^a, \ell_j))$ is the overlap between AV $a_i$'s route and PV $p_j$'s current uncovered interval $[\ell_j, x_j^p]$. The LSAP solver finds a minimum-cost one-to-one matching---equivalently, a matching that \textit{maximizes} total towed distance for the current cost matrix.

\subsubsection{State Update for Multi-Segment Iterations}

Each PV $p_j$ maintains state $(\ell_j, \hat{t}_j)$: the left boundary of its uncovered interval and its arrival time at $\ell_j$. The PV arrival time at a candidate coupling point $cp$ is:
\begin{equation}
    t_j^p(cp) = \hat{t}_j + \frac{cp - \ell_j}{v_j^p}
    \label{eq:pv_arrival}
\end{equation}
After assigning PV $p_j$ to AV $a_i$ on $[cp,dp]$, the state updates as:
\begin{align}
    \ell_j^{new} &\leftarrow dp \label{eq:state_pos}\\
    \hat{t}_j^{new} &\leftarrow \hat{t}_j^{old} + \frac{cp - \ell_j^{old}}{v_j^p} + \frac{dp - cp}{v_i^a} \label{eq:state_time}
\end{align}
where the first term in~\eqref{eq:state_time} is independent driving to the coupling point and the second is towing at AV speed.

\subsubsection{Algorithm and Line-by-Line Explanation}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:hungarian} presents the full ILA procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Iterative Linear Assignment (ILA) for Multi-AV Matching}
\label{alg:hungarian}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE AVs $\mathcal{A}$, PVs $\mathcal{P}$, minimum length $L_{min}$
\ENSURE Assignments $\mathcal{X}$, total saving
\STATE Initialize states as in Algorithm~\ref{alg:greedy}
\REPEAT
    \STATE Build cost matrix $\mathbf{W}$ from current states
    \STATE Expand AVs into $\sum_i C_i$ virtual slots
    \IF{no feasible assignments}
        \STATE \textbf{break}
    \ENDIF
    \STATE $matching \leftarrow$ \texttt{SolveLSAP}($\mathbf{W}$)
    \FOR{each $(slot, j) \in matching$ with $W_{slot,j} < +\infty$}
        \IF{capacity feasible at all critical points (Lemma~\ref{lem:critical})}
            \STATE $\mathcal{X} \leftarrow \mathcal{X} \cup \{(p_j, a_i, cp, dp)\}$;\; $total \mathrel{+}= (dp - cp)$
            \STATE Update $(\ell_j, \hat{t}_j)$ via~\eqref{eq:state_pos}--\eqref{eq:state_time}
        \ELSE
            \STATE Discard; $p_j$ remains eligible for next iteration
        \ENDIF
    \ENDFOR
\UNTIL{no new assignments}
\RETURN $\mathcal{X}$, $total$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\textbf{Walkthrough.} Line~1 initializes all PV states identically to the greedy algorithm. Lines~3--4 construct the bipartite graph: the cost matrix $\mathbf{W}$ is built from current uncovered PV intervals, and each AV is expanded into its virtual slots. Line~8 solves the LSAP on $\mathbf{W}$, returning an optimal one-to-one slot-to-PV matching for the current iteration. Lines~9--15 process each matched pair \textit{sequentially}: for each pair, we verify point-wise capacity feasibility at all critical points (Lemma~\ref{lem:critical}) given assignments already accepted \textit{within this iteration}. Feasible pairs are accepted (with saving accumulated) and PV states are updated; infeasible pairs (arising from capacity conflicts when multiple slots of the same AV are matched) are discarded, and those PVs remain eligible in subsequent iterations. The outer loop (line~2) repeats until no new feasible assignments are found, enabling multi-segment coverage across iterations.

\subsubsection{Complexity}

Let $K = \sum_{i=1}^{N} C_i$ denote the total virtual slots. Each iteration constructs a $K \times M$ cost matrix in $O(KM)$ time and solves the LSAP in $O(\max(K,M)^3)$ worst-case time. Since each accepted assignment advances at least one PV boundary, the iteration count is at most $O(NM)$, giving total worst-case complexity $O(NM \cdot \max(K,M)^3)$.

\subsection{Theoretical Analysis}

The full multi-segment problem~\eqref{eq:obj}--\eqref{eq:minlen} is NP-hard, as it generalizes GAP~\cite{cattrysse1992survey}. We therefore analyze the guarantees of our approximate algorithms.

\begin{theorem}[Per-Iteration Optimality]
\label{thm:hungarian}
At each iteration of Algorithm~\ref{alg:hungarian}, the LSAP solver returns a matching that maximizes total towed distance among all one-to-one slot-to-PV assignments for the cost matrix $\mathbf{W}$ constructed from current states.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\mathbf{W}$ encodes negated towed distances for feasible pairs and $+\infty$ otherwise. The LSAP solver finds a minimum-cost matching, which equivalently maximizes total towed distance. The Jonker--Volgenant algorithm solves LSAP exactly~\cite{burkard2012assignment}. Convergence follows because each accepted assignment strictly advances at least one PV's boundary $\ell_j$, bounding the total iterations.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Global Optimality Gap]
\label{lem:global}
Algorithm~\ref{alg:hungarian} does not guarantee global optimality for the full multi-segment problem~\eqref{eq:obj}--\eqref{eq:minlen}. Assignments accepted in iteration $r$ fix PV states for all subsequent iterations; alternative choices in round $r$ could yield higher total savings globally.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Consider $a_1$ overlapping $p_1$ on $[0,30]$ and $a_2$ on $[10,50]$. ILA may assign $p_1 \to a_1$ (saving 30), advancing $\ell_1$ to 30; the remaining $a_2$ overlap is $[30,50]$ (saving 20), for total 50. Yet assigning $p_1 \to a_2$ directly yields 40, freeing $a_1$ for other PVs---potentially producing a higher global total.
\end{proof}

Despite this gap, ILA consistently matches or exceeds greedy performance in all our experiments (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}). The iterative decomposition reduces the NP-hard multi-segment problem to a polynomial sequence of tractable LSAP subproblems.

\subsection{Discussion}

\textbf{Methodological Implications:} The key value of ILA is structural rather than speed claims: each iteration solves its assignment subproblem optimally, virtual-slot expansion encodes AV capacity directly, and the iterative state update supports multi-segment matching while preserving feasibility constraints.

\textbf{Runtime Observation (Empirical):} In our Python/SciPy implementation, ILA shows lower wall-clock runtime than greedy in tested settings. This behavior is implementation-dependent rather than a theoretical runtime guarantee. Likely contributors include:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \texttt{scipy.optimize.linear\_sum\_assignment} uses highly optimized compiled C code with efficient memory access patterns
    \item Greedy requires repeated sorting ($O(NM \log NM)$ per iteration) and candidate regeneration after each assignment
    \item ILA processes all assignments in batches, amortizing overhead
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Capacity Impact:} Doubling capacity yields sub-linear improvement (24.8\% $\rightarrow$ 52.3\%), suggesting diminishing returns. Fleet operators should balance capacity costs against marginal savings.

\textbf{Length Effect:} Saving percentage drops from 31.6\% (L=50) to 7.5\% (L=1600) as vehicles have less overlap on longer highways. This suggests our approach is most effective for medium-length corridors with high vehicle density.

\subsection{Limitations}

Our results should be interpreted within four scope boundaries: (1) a one-dimensional highway abstraction rather than a full road network, (2) a distance-based saving proxy instead of a physics-level power model, (3) per-iteration optimality for ILA without global optimality guarantees for the full multi-segment objective, and (4) an offline setting with complete trajectory knowledge. These assumptions keep the analysis tractable and define the applicability of the reported performance.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a dynamic towing-based platoon formation framework for heterogeneous autonomous vehicles and formulated it as a constrained multi-segment matching problem on a one-dimensional corridor. Across scenarios with up to 80 AVs and 400 PVs, both algorithms achieved substantial coverage gains (25--52\%), while ILA consistently matched or exceeded greedy solution quality. The framework captures point-wise capacity and temporal synchronization constraints, and the theoretical scope is explicit: ILA guarantees per-iteration optimality for each LSAP subproblem but not global optimality for the full multi-segment objective.
